# B2B SaaS Content Frameworks
A comprehensive reference library of the most important content frameworks used by B2B SaaS companies for LinkedIn organic growth and broader content strategy.

## Framework 1: The Content Pillar System
**Source:** Justin Welsh (adapted widely across the industry)
**What it is:** A method for organizing all LinkedIn content around 3–5 core topic areas ("pillars") that define your expertise and attract your ideal audience.
**How it works:**
**Step 1 — Define your pillars**
Choose 3–5 topics that:
- You have genuine expertise in
- Your target audience cares about
- Are specific enough to build authority (not "marketing" — instead "LinkedIn content strategy for Series A SaaS founders")
**Example pillars for a SaaS founder:**
1. LinkedIn organic growth for B2B founders
2. Building a remote-first sales team
3. SaaS pricing strategy
4. Founder mental health and resilience
5. Product-led growth for early-stage SaaS
**Step 2 — Create content within pillars**
Every post should belong to one pillar. This consistency:
- Signals to the algorithm what your account is "about"
- Trains your audience to expect certain topics from you
- Makes content batching dramatically easier
**Step 3 — Rotate pillars over a week**
A sample weekly rotation:
- Monday: Pillar 1 (LinkedIn growth)
- Tuesday: Pillar 4 (founder mental health)
- Wednesday: Pillar 2 (sales team building)
- Thursday: Pillar 3 (SaaS pricing)
- Friday: Pillar 5 (PLG) or personal story crossing multiple pillars

## Framework 2: Hook → Teach → Ask (HTA)
**Source:** Justin Welsh
**What it is:** The three-part structure that governs every high-performing LinkedIn post.
**The components:**
**Hook (Line 1):** The single sentence that determines whether anyone reads the rest. Must stop the scroll, create curiosity or tension, and make a clear promise.
Hook formula options:
- Contrarian claim: "Most [common belief] is wrong. Here's the truth:"
- Specific number: "I [did X thing] for [time period]. Here's what happened:"
- Personal transformation: "2 years ago [negative situation]. Today [positive outcome]. The difference:"
- Question: "What if [common assumption] is actually [counterintuitive conclusion]?"
**Teach (Lines 2–8):** The body of the post. Delivers the promised value with:
- Short paragraphs (max 2 sentences)
- Specific examples, not generic advice
- Numbered or bulleted lists for complex information
- White space between every paragraph
**Ask (Final Line):** The call to action. One specific ask:
- Follow: "Follow me for more frameworks like this."
- Comment: "Which of these do you use? Comment below."
- DM: "DM me [keyword] for the full template."
- Link: "Full breakdown in the first comment."

## Framework 4: The Demand Generation Flywheel
**Source:** Chris Walker / Refine Labs
**What it is:** A model for how LinkedIn organic content creates compounding demand in B2B SaaS — driving pipeline through dark social and brand awareness rather than direct lead capture.
**The flywheel stages:**
```
LinkedIn content (daily) 
→ builds brand awareness in your ICP
→ ICP shares content in dark social (Slack, DMs, email)
→ referral traffic arrives at your website (shows as "Direct")
→ inbound pipeline increases
→ success enables more content investment
→ flywheel accelerates
```
**Measurement protocol:**
1. Self-reported attribution question on demo forms: "How did you first hear about us?"
2. Pipeline interviews with won customers: "What content touched you before booking a call?"
3. Brand search volume tracking (Google Search Console, Ahrefs)
4. Inbound pipeline trend (month-over-month, correlated with content publishing volume)

## Framework 5: The B2B Brand Voice Framework
**Source:** Dave Gerhardt / Exit Five
**What it is:** A set of criteria for evaluating whether B2B SaaS content sounds like a human being or a corporate committee.
**The Brand Voice Test — 5 Questions:**
1. **Contractions test:** Does the content use contractions (it's, we're, you'll)? If it sounds formal and stiff, rewrite.
2. **Position test:** Does the content take a clear side? Or does it hedge with "it depends"? Great brand voice picks a side.
3. **Specificity test:** Does the content include specific examples, numbers, or stories? Or does it make vague claims? Be specific.
4. **Uniqueness test:** Could this content have been published by any competitor? If yes, rewrite until only your company could have published it.
5. **Read-aloud test:** Read the post aloud. Does it sound like something a human being would say in a meeting or phone call?
**The Brand Voice Persona Framework:**
Define your B2B brand voice with a 2×2 matrix:
- X-axis: Formal ←→ Casual
- Y-axis: Serious ←→ Playful
Position your brand in one quadrant and ensure all content is created within that quadrant.

## Framework 6: The Show Me You Know Me (SMYKM) Framework
**Source:** Sam McKenna / #samsales
**What it is:** A personalization framework for B2B LinkedIn outreach that dramatically increases reply rates by demonstrating genuine research.
**The 5-point research checklist:**
1. Activity tab review (2 min): What has the prospect been engaging with recently?
2. Post history review (2 min): What has the prospect been publishing?
3. About section (1 min): What mission, values, and career trajectory do they emphasize?
4. Shared connections (1 min): Who do you know in common?
5. Company news (3 min): What has happened at their company in the last 90 days?
**The SMYKM message formula:**
```
[Specific observation about their situation] + 
[Genuine insight connecting to that observation] + 
[One low-friction question or offer]
```

## Framework 10: The Revenue Vitals Measurement System
**Source:** Chris Walker / Refine Labs
**What it is:** A set of leading and lagging indicators for measuring the business impact of LinkedIn organic content in B2B SaaS.
**Leading indicators (measure monthly):**
- LinkedIn follower growth rate
- Content engagement rate (comments + reposts ÷ impressions)
- Email newsletter open rate and growth
- Brand search volume (Google Search Console)
- Inbound DM volume
**Lagging indicators (measure quarterly):**
- Inbound pipeline volume (correlated with content investment)
- Pipeline close rate for inbound leads
- Deal velocity (time-to-close for content-influenced deals)
- Customer acquisition cost (trend line)
- Self-reported attribution to LinkedIn
**Implementation:** Create a monthly "LinkedIn Dashboard" in Google Sheets or Notion tracking all 10 metrics. Review quarterly to identify content investment vs. pipeline correlation.

# Books, Podcasts & Additional Resources
A curated reference list of books, podcasts, newsletters, communities, and tools relevant to LinkedIn organic content strategy for B2B SaaS.
---
## Essential Books
### 1. Jab, Jab, Jab, Right Hook — Gary Vaynerchuk
**Buy:** [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Jab-Right-Hook-Tell-Social/dp/006227306X)
**Relevance:** The foundational framework for give-give-give-ask content strategy. The "jab" = value-giving content. The "right hook" = the ask (CTA). Directly applicable to LinkedIn content cadence for B2B founders.
**Key lesson:** Give value relentlessly before asking for anything. The ratio of value-giving to asking determines long-term audience loyalty.
### 2. Building a StoryBrand — Donald Miller
**Buy:** [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Building-StoryBrand-Clarify-Message-Customers/dp/0718033329)
**Relevance:** Clarifies how to frame your B2B SaaS messaging so that the customer is the hero — not your company. Directly applicable to LinkedIn content that attracts buyers rather than alienating them.
**Key lesson:** Position your customer as the hero, your product as the guide. All great B2B content follows this structure.
### 3. Obviously Awesome — April Dunford
**Buy:** [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Obviously-Awesome-Product-Positioning-Customers/dp/1999529065)
**Relevance:** The definitive book on B2B SaaS product positioning. Positioning determines what LinkedIn content to create — your content should reinforce your positioning, not undermine it.
**Key lesson:** Great positioning makes all marketing easier — including LinkedIn content. Start with positioning, then write content.
### 4. Traction — Gabriel Weinberg & Justin Mares
**Buy:** [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Traction-Startup-Achieve-Explosive-Customer/dp/1591848369)
**Relevance:** Covers 19 customer acquisition channels including content marketing and social media. Provides the framework for testing LinkedIn organic as a traction channel alongside paid, SEO, and community.
**Key lesson:** Systematically test LinkedIn organic as one of 19 traction channels — then double down on the 2–3 that show the highest ROI.
### 5. Contagious — Jonah Berger
**Buy:** [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Contagious-Things-Catch-Jonah-Berger/dp/1451686579)
**Relevance:** Explains the science behind why content spreads. The STEPPS framework (Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories) directly applies to LinkedIn content that gets shared and commented on.
**Key lesson:** Content spreads when it gives people social currency, triggers emotion, and provides practical value — design B2B LinkedIn posts with these drivers.
### 6. This Is Marketing — Seth Godin
**Buy:** [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/This-Marketing-Cant-Until-Learn/dp/0525540830)
**Relevance:** Godin's philosophy of "smallest viable audience" maps directly to the specificity-first approach for LinkedIn content. Argues for finding the smallest group that could change everything, not marketing to everyone.
**Key lesson:** LinkedIn content that resonates with 1,000 exactly right people is more valuable than content that vaguely interests 100,000.
### 7. Demand-Side Sales 101 — Bob Moesta
**Buy:** [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Demand-Side-Sales-101-Customer-Progress/dp/1544509987)
**Relevance:** Introduces Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) thinking for B2B sales. Deeply relevant for creating LinkedIn content that speaks to the "jobs" your buyers are trying to get done — not just the features of your product.
**Key lesson:** Write LinkedIn content about the buyer's struggle and desired outcome, not about your product's capabilities.

## How the LinkedIn Algorithm Works (2024–2025)
### Overview
LinkedIn's algorithm uses a multi-stage content distribution system. Understanding each stage allows B2B creators to optimize their posts for maximum organic reach.
---
### Stage 1: Initial Distribution (First 60 Minutes)
When you publish a post, LinkedIn distributes it to a small sample of your followers — approximately **1–3% of your total follower count**.
**How engagement in this window determines reach:**
- High engagement (>2% engagement rate) → algorithm expands distribution
- Low engagement (<0.5% engagement rate) → algorithm limits further distribution
- Comments are weighted significantly higher than likes or reactions
**What this means:** The first 60 minutes after publishing are critical. Post when your audience is active, and reply to every early comment immediately to signal engagement to the algorithm.
---

---
### Stage 3: Human Review (for Viral Content)
If a post performs exceptionally in stages 1 and 2, LinkedIn may conduct a human quality review before expanding distribution further. This filters out:
- Spam or engagement bait ("Comment YES if you agree")
- Misleading content
- Low-quality, repetitive posts
**Implication:** Authentic, high-value content passes human review. Engagement bait does not.
---
### Key Algorithm Rules for B2B LinkedIn Content
#### Rule 1: External Links Reduce Reach
LinkedIn penalizes posts containing external URLs (to reduce the chance users leave the platform).
**Solution:** Always put links in the first comment, not the post body. Reference the comment in your post: "Full framework in the first comment."
**Estimated reach impact:** Posts with external links receive 50–80% less organic reach than equivalent link-free posts.
#### Rule 2: Creator Mode Matters
Enabling LinkedIn Creator Mode:
- Shows your follower count on your profile (social proof)
- Enables LinkedIn Newsletter (direct inbox reach to followers)
- Gives access to LinkedIn Live and Audio Events
- May provide slight algorithmic preference for creator accounts
**Action:** Enable Creator Mode in LinkedIn Settings → Visibility → Creator Mode.
#### Rule 3: Posting Frequency Baseline
LinkedIn's algorithm establishes a "posting rhythm" for each account. Consistent daily or near-daily posting signals an active account and receives preferential distribution.
**Recommended minimum:** 3 posts per week to maintain algorithmic momentum.
**Recommended optimal:** 5 posts per week (Mon–Fri) for B2B founders.
Posting less than once per week causes an algorithmic "cooldown" — your next post will receive less distribution than if you'd posted consistently.
#### Rule 4: Native Content Is Preferred
LinkedIn prefers content created natively on the platform:
- Native videos outperform shared YouTube links
- LinkedIn carousels (PDF documents) get high dwell time credit
- LinkedIn polls generate high engagement signals
- LinkedIn articles get less feed distribution but rank in LinkedIn search
**Priority order for B2B content:** Carousel > Native video > Text post > Text + image > Article > External link post
#### Rule 5: Comment Quality Matters
A comment saying "Great post!" carries almost no algorithmic weight.
A comment with 2–3 sentences adding genuine insight significantly boosts distribution.
**For your own posts:** Reply to every comment with a substantive response. Each reply counts as new engagement and resets the algorithm's attention on your post.
**For others' posts:** Leave comments longer than "Great point!" — add your own perspective, data, or follow-up question.

### Key Insights
#### 1. The Authenticity Paradox in B2B
Amelia identifies the central paradox of B2B personal branding: the content that feels most "professional" and "safe" generates the least trust, engagement, and business impact. The content that feels most vulnerable and personal generates the most.
**The paradox explained:**
- Professional content (product benefits, company wins, industry news): 200 impressions, 5 likes
- Personal content (a business failure, a health scare, a mistake made publicly): 20,000 impressions, 400 comments, 15 DMs
**Why this happens:** B2B buyers are humans first, buyers second. They trust people they feel they know. Authentic personal content creates a sense of knowing someone faster than any professional content can.

### Key Insights
#### 1. The Blank Page Problem and How to Defeat It
Jasmin identifies the #1 reason LinkedIn creators give up: the blank page. His solution is a "brain dump → structure → hook" process:
**Step 1 — Brain dump:** Write everything you know about a topic in 10 minutes. No editing, no structure, no judgment.
**Step 2 — Find the core:** Read the brain dump and circle the single most interesting or surprising sentence.
**Step 3 — Build the structure:** Use that sentence as your "north star" and organize everything else around it.
**Step 4 — Write the hook last:** After the full post is written, distill its most essential message into one magnetic first line.
This process turns "I don't know what to write" into a systematic, repeatable creation practice.
#### 2. The Rhythm Rule
Jasmin teaches that great LinkedIn writing has a reading rhythm — like music. Readers subconsciously feel when writing flows vs. when it stumbles.
**The Rhythm Formula:**
- Alternate between short and medium-length sentences
- Never use three sentences of the same length in a row
- Use single-word sentences ("Really." "Always." "Never.") for emphasis at key moments
- Create deliberate contrast: fast → slow → fast
**Example of rhythm done right:**
```
Most people post on LinkedIn and wonder why nobody reads it.
I did the same for three months.
Then I discovered one thing.
The sentences were too long.
Every. Single. One.
Shorter sentences create momentum. Readers move forward. They can't stop.
Long sentences — like this one that goes on and describes many different things in sequence — actually make readers stop and re-read, which breaks flow and encourages them to scroll away.
See the difference?
```

#### 4. White Space as a Strategic Tool
Jasmin dedicates an entire section to white space (line breaks) — arguing it is as important as the words themselves.
**The white space rules:**
1. Maximum 2 short sentences per paragraph
2. Use a line break every time you introduce a new idea
3. Single-line paragraphs are allowed and encouraged for emphasis
4. Never let a paragraph block exceed 4 lines on mobile
**Why it matters:** White space reduces reading friction. When the eye sees manageable chunks of text, the brain says "I can read this." When it sees a dense paragraph, the brain says "This looks hard" and scrolls away.
#### 5. The Edit-Down Process
Jasmin argues that the first draft of any LinkedIn post is always too long. His edit-down rule: after writing your first draft, cut 30% of the words before publishing.
**What to cut:**
- "In conclusion," / "To summarize," — delete entirely
- Adverbs (very, really, quite, basically) — delete almost all of them
- Filler phrases ("I think that," "It's important to note that,") — cut to the core claim
- Repeated ideas — if you said it, don't say it again differently
- Weak qualifiers ("kind of," "somewhat," "in a way")

**Phase 2 — Human for opinion:** Write your personal take, specific examples, and contrarian opinions yourself. This is the 20% that AI cannot generate — your proprietary perspective.
**Phase 3 — AI for structure:** Paste your human-written core ideas into AI and ask it to suggest a post structure. Evaluate the suggestions critically — take what works, discard what sounds generic.
**Phase 4 — Human for hook and voice:** Always write your hook and final edit yourself. The hook must sound like you. AI hooks sound like AI.
**Phase 5 — Human for final edit:** Read the post aloud. If any sentence sounds like a robot wrote it, rewrite it.
#### The Voice Fingerprint
Jasmin introduces the concept of a "voice fingerprint" — the set of characteristics that make your writing immediately recognizable as yours:
- Your typical sentence length pattern
- Words and phrases you use frequently
- The way you open posts
- Your emotional register (serious, playful, provocative, warm)
- The topics you return to repeatedly
Once you know your voice fingerprint, you can train AI to write in that style — or at minimum, identify when AI output has drifted away from it.

2. **Write your personal opinion and specific examples before touching AI** — protect your proprietary insights.
3. **Always read AI-assisted posts aloud** — if it sounds robotic, rewrite it.

5. **Final edit is always human** — especially the hook and the closing sentence.

3. **Blog posts without distribution plans** — content published once and never redistributed.
4. **Vanity metrics optimization** — content optimized for likes and impressions rather than pipeline and brand authority.
---
### Important Quotes
> "The B2B companies winning in content aren't creating more. They're creating with a distribution system and an original point of view. Both are required."
> "If your content could have been written by any competitor, it's not differentiated enough. Put your proprietary research, your client data, and your specific opinion in every piece."

### Actionable Takeaways
1. **Prioritize original research** — even a simple survey of 100 customers generates unique, linkable data.
2. **Ungate your best content** — trust > email capture in modern B2B buying journeys.
3. **Every post needs a distribution plan** — publish nowhere without a 3-month redistribution schedule.
4. **Use AI for drafts, humans for voice** — never publish AI-first content without substantial human editing.

### Key Insights
#### 1. What SMYKM Means and Why It Works
"Show Me You Know Me" is Sam's core philosophy for all B2B outreach: before asking for anything, demonstrate that you've done the work to understand this specific person's world.
**The psychology behind SMYKM:**
- Every human being has a fundamental need to feel seen and understood
- Generic outreach communicates "I see you as a transaction"
- Personalized outreach communicates "I see you as a person"
- The feeling of being genuinely seen is rare enough in B2B that it creates immediate differentiation
**What "knowing" someone means in the context of LinkedIn:**
1. You've read their recent LinkedIn posts (and can reference a specific insight from one)
2. You know about their company's recent news (funding, product launch, hiring)
3. You share a mutual connection who is genuine (not just a name to drop)
4. You understand the specific challenges of their role at their stage of company
#### 2. The LinkedIn Research Checklist (Pre-Outreach)
Sam shares the 5-point LinkedIn research protocol she trains all SDRs on:
**Step 1 — Activity Tab Review (3 min):**
Read the last 5 posts the prospect liked, commented on, or shared. What topics are they engaging with? What opinions are they expressing? This tells you their professional priorities right now.
**Step 2 — Post History Review (2 min):**
Read the last 3–5 posts the prospect personally published. What are they teaching? What challenges are they sharing? This reveals their current professional narrative.
**Step 3 — About Section (1 min):**
Read their About section for: career trajectory, personal mission statement, values they emphasize. These often contain phrases you can reflect back in your opening.
**Step 4 — Shared Connections (1 min):**
Identify any mutual first-degree connections who are genuine relationships (not just connected). A warm referral from a shared connection is 10× more effective than cold SMYKM.
**Step 5 — Company News (2 min):**
Google "[Company Name] news" and filter to last 90 days. Funding rounds, product launches, executive hires, and press mentions give you current conversation hooks.
**Total research time: 9 minutes per prospect**
**Expected reply rate improvement: 300–500% vs. generic templates**
#### 3. The SMYKM Opening Formula
Sam provides her exact formula for a SMYKM LinkedIn DM opening:
```
[Observation about their specific situation] + 
[Genuine insight or connection to that observation] + 
[One low-friction question]
```
**Bad example (generic):**
"Hi [Name], I came across your profile and thought you might be interested in our sales training solution. Can we set up a call?"
**Good example (SMYKM):**
"Hi [Name], I saw your post last week about the challenge of building SDR confidence in Q4 — resonated with a lot of what we hear from mid-stage SaaS sales leaders. I've been working with a few teams on exactly this and have one framework that's been working really well. Would it be useful if I shared it?"
**The difference:**
- The SMYKM version references a specific post
- It demonstrates understanding of the prospect's role and stage
- It offers value before asking for anything
- The question requires only a yes/no — minimal friction
#### 4. LinkedIn Content as a Warm Outreach Multiplier
Sam makes a crucial connection between personal LinkedIn content and outreach effectiveness:
**The mechanism:**
- SDR or AE posts on LinkedIn 3×/week about relevant B2B challenges
- Prospect sees the content, recognizes the SDR's name
- SDR sends cold email / LinkedIn DM to prospect
- Prospect sees familiar name and content creator → higher open and reply rate
Sam shares data: reps who post consistently on LinkedIn see 30–50% higher reply rates to cold outreach than reps who don't — same company, same product, same ICP.
**Why this works:**
- Content creates the feeling of familiarity before the first direct contact
- Familiarity reduces the psychological "stranger danger" response to cold outreach
- The prospect thinks: "I've seen this person's ideas before. They know their stuff."
